Why We Give Flowers? The Story of an Ancient Ritual

I have always wondered why giving flowers feels like such a universal language - why a simple bouquet can say “I love you,” “I am sorry,” “I miss you,” or even “Goodbye,” without using a single word. I thought it would be a straightforward story about romance or beauty. Instead, I stumbled into one of the oldest rituals of humanity - a tradition so ancient that it began long before we had poetry, alphabets, or even cities.

The earliest evidence of people giving flowers dates back over 13,000 years. In prehistoric graves, archaeologists found carefully arranged blossoms laid beside the dead. These were not offerings of decoration. They were messages. A way of saying: We remember you. We honor you. We still feel something for you. Even then, flowers were emotional objects - fragile, temporary, and painfully beautiful. When ancient civilizations began to rise, the meaning of flowers grew with them. The Egyptians offered lotus blossoms to the sun god Ra because they believed the petals opened with the sunrise. To give someone a lotus was to wish them rebirth, protection, or divine favor. The Greeks used flowers as symbols in their myths - roses for Aphrodite, laurel for Apollo - and people began wearing them as crowns to borrow the gods’ qualities. Imagine giving someone a flower not just for beauty, but for power: courage, love, clarity, victory. Flowers were not gifts; they were spells.

Later, in medieval and Persian traditions, the ritual became even more sophisticated. Entire conversations happened through bouquets. A single bloom could deliver a secret message. A red rose meant desire. Basil meant hatred. Ivy meant loyalty. And if you sent someone a yellow carnation… well, you were basically telling them “I like you, but not like that.” Even now, there is something thrilling about the idea that flowers can speak when we can not. But the deeper reason we give flowers goes beyond symbolism. Psychologists suggest it taps into a primal part of us - the part that responds to color, fragility, scent, and impermanence. Flowers remind us that beauty does not last. They give us something to hold in the present moment, something delicate and alive. When you hand someone a bouquet, you are offering them a moment of beauty that will fade - and that is the whole point. It says: this moment matters because it will never exist again.

And maybe that is why flowers have survived every era, every culture, every language. We give them at births, weddings, celebrations, funerals. We give them in joy and grief, in confession and apology. Flowers are the closest thing we have to emotional shorthand - a ritual that began in the soil of ancient graves and still blooms in our modern hands. Even as I write this, I keep thinking about the strange, beautiful contradiction that flowers carry. They die quickly, yet they are one of the most meaningful gifts we offer. Maybe that is exactly why they matter. They remind us that life is temporary, and connection is precious. And when we give flowers, we are not really giving plants - we are offering presence, intention, and a little piece of our human story.

Mirror. Why People Feared Their Own Reflection

I will admit something: even as the author of this article, someone who has spent weeks researching the cultural history of mirrors, I still hesitate to look at my reflection late at night. Maybe it is the silence. Maybe it is the dim light. Or maybe it is the trace of old Turkic traditions I grew up hearing - the ones that warn you never to look into a mirror after midnight because the veil between you and “the other world” becomes thin. Whatever the reason, the feeling is real. And as it turns out, I am far from alone.

For thousands of years, humans approached mirrors with a mix of awe, curiosity, and fear. Long before mirrors became a casual part of bathrooms and bedroom walls, the very idea of seeing one’s own face reflected in something still and silent was almost supernatural. Ancient cultures believed reflections were not just images but extensions of the soul. A mirror was not a tool - it was a threshold, a fragile surface separating the world of the living from something deeper. In many early civilizations, people avoided mirrors during sickness, mourning, or nighttime. Why? Because they thought the reflection could capture a weakened soul and pull it away. In some traditions, mirrors were covered when someone died to prevent the spirit from getting trapped inside. And though modern psychology might explain these fears away, the emotional core remains strangely understandable. There is something uncanny about meeting your own eyes in a cold glass that does not blink. We found that nearly every culture had a version of this fear. The Greeks believed mirrors could reveal omens. Medieval Europeans avoided them during thunderstorms, thinking lightning could “ignite” the spirit inside the reflection. In Slavic and Turkic folklore, mirrors after sunset were considered openings - porous, watchful. My grandmother used to say, half-joking and half-serious, that after midnight a mirror does not show you, it shows who is watching you. And even though I am a grown adult writing a cultural analysis, I still catch myself turning the mirror to the wall if I feel uneasy at night.

But fear of mirrors was not only mystical. There is also something deeply psychological happening. To see yourself as another being - detached, framed, silent - creates a small shock. A moment where your identity becomes double. A version of you exists “out there,” inside that glass world, and you cannot control it. Even today, that sensation can feel unsettling. Especially when the room is dark and the mirror seems too deep, too reflective, too still. And maybe that is why mirrors have never been “just objects.” They have been instruments of prophecy, tools of vanity, portals in myth, symbols in literature, and quiet witnesses in every bedroom. They hide nothing, but they also reveal things we do not always want to see - not supernatural creatures, but our own truths, anxieties, and unfiltered selves. So yes, humans feared mirrors for thousands of years. Maybe because they saw spirits. Maybe because they sensed the uncanny. Maybe because they recognized something intimate and ancient: that a reflection is not only an image, but a moment of encounter. A meeting with the self you cannot escape. And if I still sometimes avoid looking into the mirror at 3 a.m., maybe I am simply continuing a very old human tradition - one that reminds me that even the most ordinary objects can carry centuries of myth, memory, and mystery inside them.

An Underrated Genius of His Time: Van Gogh and His Tragic Fate

Long before his paintings lit up museum walls, Vincent van Gogh was a restless dropout wandering through Europe with pockets of lint and a head full of questions. He left school early, struggled to hold jobs, and drifted from art dealing to preaching to manual labor. Nothing fit. Yet in two of his early letters to his brother Theo, we glimpse a young man slowly discovering the courage to name his true calling.

A Spark in His Letters

In the first letter, Vincent confesses his confusion about the future, sensing a creative force rising inside him but not yet knowing how to direct it. His voice is raw, urgent, almost pleading. It’s the prelude to transformation - the moment before the leap. In the second, the hesitation fades. Van Gogh boldly calls himself an artist, even though he is still a beginner and painfully aware of it. This letter marks the turning point: the instant he chooses art not as a hobby, but as a destiny.

A Life Caught Between Light and Shadow

Born in the Netherlands in 1853, Van Gogh lived intensely, often too intensely for those around him. His emotional highs and crushing lows—today often understood as symptoms of mental illness - shaped both his personal life and his art. Periods of isolation, anxiety, and confusion frequently collided with moments of astonishing creative clarity. His stay in Arles, his bond and eventual rupture with Gauguin, and his time in the Saint-Rémy asylum were all chapters in a life where creativity and suffering coexisted, feeding and fighting each other in equal measure.

An Artist the World Ignored

Despite his relentless work—over 2,000 artworks in less than a decade—Van Gogh sold almost nothing during his lifetime. Critics dismissed him as unstable; his bold colors and swirling brushwork were considered strange, even grotesque. He painted masterpieces in isolation, believing, with heartbreaking accuracy, that the world might never understand him. Yet these same techniques would later ignite modern art, giving birth to expressionism and shaping the visual language of the 20th century.

The Legacy Hidden in Two Letters

These two early letters reveal Van Gogh before Starry Night, before the sunflowers, before fame: a young man wrestling with doubt, mental turmoil, and financial hardship - but also a relentless, almost heroic commitment to creation. The world only recognized his genius after he was gone. But in these letters, we see the first sparks of that genius - fragile, flickering, but impossible to extinguish.

What the Word “Beauty” Hides: Traces of Ancient Goddesses

When we say beauty today, we imagine something soft, harmless, almost decorative. But the ancient world saw beauty as something far more powerful-divine, dangerous, transformative. Behind the word stand two unforgettable figures: Aphrodite of Greece and Freya of the Norse north. Their stories reveal the deeper, wilder meanings beauty once carried.

Aphrodite: The Birth of Desire

According to Greek myth, Aphrodite rose from the foam of the Mediterranean, a symbol of life erupting from the sea. Yet her beauty was never simple. She embodied charis-the Greek idea of magnetic charm that could soothe conflict or spark catastrophe. Historically, Aphrodite’s temples stretched from Cyprus to Corinth, acting not just as religious centers but as cultural hubs where rituals of fertility, marriage, and community took place. Her influence seeped into politics, art, and even city identity. Beauty, in her world, shaped societies.

Freya: Beauty as Magic and Strength

In the northern sagas, beauty arrives with a different temperature. Freya, goddess of love and desire, also wielded seidr, a powerful magical art that could twist destiny. She was a warrior’s ally, a chooser of the slain, and a diplomat among gods and giants. Archaeological finds-from amber pendants to falcon-feather motifs-suggest that Freya’s presence in Viking culture blended beauty with protection, magic, and leadership. Her allure wasn’t passive; it commanded respect.

Beauty in the Modern Mirror

Today, beauty is still a force-only the goddesses have been replaced by screens. Social media, advertising, and celebrity culture shape ideals with the same intensity ancient myths once did. But while Aphrodite and Freya embodied beauty as power, creativity, and identity, modern beauty standards often shrink it into something fragile and standardized. Yet the old echoes remain. Our fascination with transformation, charisma, and personal magnetism shows we still chase something deeper than symmetry. We still treat beauty as a language-a way to express identity, attract connection, or reshape how others see us. The ancient goddesses remind us that beauty was never meant to be small. It was meant to be expansive, influential, and alive-just as it is today, when we choose to see it beyond the filters.

Why We Fall in Love With the Unavailable - Psychology Meets Mythology

It is one of those mysteries that has followed humanity for as long as stories have existed: why do we so often fall for the distant, the untouchable, the impossible? And the deeper we went into research, the clearer it became - this attraction is woven into both our psychology and our oldest myths.

Psychologists say that the allure of the unavailable often begins with projection. When someone is distant, emotionally guarded, or simply out of reach, our minds fill in the empty spaces with idealized versions of who they might be. We imagine a perfect listener, a passionate lover, a soulmate who understands us in ways no one else does. In reality, we are falling in love not with the person, but with the potential - a character we script in our own heads. Yet this illusion can feel intoxicatingly real, precisely because nothing in the real world interrupts it.

At the same time, mythology has been whispering the same story for thousands of years. We have always been drawn to gods and spirits who stand just outside the human world - too brilliant to touch, too dangerous to hold. Think of Eros, who appears and disappears like a fever dream; of Selene reaching for Endymion while he sleeps forever; of nymphs who flee the moment love calls their name; of mortals who crave divine affection even when it leads to ruin. The unattainable is the oldest archetype of desire. It promises transformation, transcendence, a life bigger than the one we already know. Unavailability creates a powerful emotional chase. When affection becomes scarce, like a rare resource, our brains assign it higher value.

We want what we cannot have because we are wired to work for what feels scarce. The harder the pursuit, the more meaningful the prize seems. And so longing becomes its own kind of addiction - a private myth we replay until it burns itself out. But perhaps the most striking discovery is this: the fascination with the unavailable is not a flaw. It is a compass pointing toward something deeper within us. Every unreachable person we fall for reflects a part of ourselves we have not yet claimed - a desire we have not voiced, a boundary we have not set, a love we have not learned to give ourselves. Mythology did not create this pattern; it simply recognized it first. The stories remind us that chasing the unreachable can teach us what we truly hunger for.

So the next time your heart aches for someone you cannot quite touch, remember: you are not alone, you are not foolish, and you are not broken. You are simply echoing a very old human rhythm - a rhythm sung by ancient gods, whispered by forgotten lovers, and studied by our team with a mix of curiosity and awe. Unavailability, it turns out, is not just a romantic trap. It is a mirror. And sometimes, it shows us more truth than any attainable love ever could.

Why People Keep Letters - The Story of Forgotten Confessions

In an age of disappearing messages and recycled chats, it might seem strange that people still keep old letters tucked into drawers, boxes, and forgotten corners of wardrobes. Yet they remain-folded, yellowing, fragile, carrying the weight of voices that no longer speak aloud. What makes letters so difficult to throw away? Why do we keep them long after relationships fade, relatives pass, and memories shift? Because letters are the last form of emotion that refuses to vanish.

Ink That Does Not Disappear

A letter is not just words; it is the writer’s breathing rhythm preserved in ink. The trembling line reveals fear. The hurried stroke betrays longing. The careful curve expresses tenderness. Psychologists say handwriting activates emotional memory more deeply than typed text. The brain reads not just the message, but the gestures behind it. That is why a single handwritten letter can resurrect a person more powerfully than a thousand digital messages. A letter is not read - it is felt.

The Historical Weight of Paper

Letters once shaped history. Napoleon wrote Josephine feverish notes of desire even from battlefields. The love letters of Tolstoy and Sofia reveal storms behind genius. World War II soldiers folded their lives into envelopes before stepping into the unknown. Many of these letters now lie in museums and archives, preserving emotions that would have vanished if left to spoken words alone. Whole eras are known to us because someone kept a letter instead of throwing it away.

Distance Makes the Heart Write Longer

Before planes, phones, and internet, a letter was the only bridge between divided lives. Lovers torn apart by war, families scattered by migration, friends separated by oceans poured their loneliness and hope onto paper. A letter arrived as a miracle - proof that the person on the other side of the map was still alive, still thinking of you, still yours in some small way.

The Confessions Between the Lines

The most powerful letters rarely say everything directly.They hesitate. They leave hints, gaps, silences. A polite phrase hides longing. A short paragraph hides regret. A crossed-out word hides a truth too sharp to reveal. These unspoken layers are why we keep letters—they contain secrets even we didn’t recognize at the time. Letters are emotional palimpsests: every reading reveals a new meaning. In a world where everything disappears with a tap, a letter is the last stubborn proof that something mattered.
